City of Toronto Open Data

Green Streets

This dataset depicts all public roads incorporating green infrastructure into their design. The main objective of green infrastructure is to curb the harmful environmental effects associated with extreme weather events, such as heavy rainfall and heatwaves. Green street projects particularly seeking to alleviate the urban heat island effect may use green infrastructure geared towards improving air quality and solar reflectivity, including green walls, soil cells for street trees, photocatalytic paving, and cool pavements.

Green Roofs

The green roofs dataset includes all structures containing green roofs and cool roofs. Green roofs consist of vegetated surfaces with various soil layers, while cool roofs instead utilize materials with high solar reflectivity and thermal emittance. Green roofs therefore have the additional benefit of effectively regulating stormwater management. Overall, both green and cool roofs help mitigate urban heat by reflecting larger amounts of sunlight, which further enhances cooling efficiency and reduces energy costs for buildings city-wide.

Green Spaces

The green spaces dataset provides the locations of parks and open spaces maintained by both the public and private sector. These areas include public parks, ravine corridors, cemeteries, and other vegetated spaces. In addition to their recreational and cultural functions, these green landscapes form a critical network that provides ecosystem benefits, including relief from extreme heat.

School of Cities

Heat Vulnerability

The heat vulnerability map reveals the uneven spatial distribution of extreme heat impacts across the population of Toronto. At its core, heat vulnerability is the product of heat exposure, heat sensitivity and adaptive capacity. Heat exposure represents the cumulative metric of surface temperatures above 20°C over the 2010 - 2019 period, computed for each pixel. On the other hand, heat sensitivity and adaptive capacity influence the severity of heat exposure by either amplifying or reducing its impacts, respectively. Heat sensitivity examines contextual markers like tree canopy cover, impervious surfaces and population age while adaptive capacity considers the socioeconomic factors contributing to a household's ability to protect itself from the adverse effects of heat exposure. This dataset was obtained from the research project Mapping Heat Vulnerability in Toronto, conducted by the School of Cities, which aims to identify communities and neighborhoods that are disproportionately vulnerable to the effects of extreme heat.
